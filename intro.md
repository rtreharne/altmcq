Good afternoon, everyone, and thank you for coming.

Multiple-choice questions are useful as a way of assessing our students.

They are efficient. They are scalable. They are easy to mark.

They can also be genuinely valuable for learning, particularly when students use them regularly to practise, test themselves, and identify gaps in their understanding.

But generative AI has changed the context in which we use them.

A student can now paste many MCQ questions into an AI tool and receive a plausible answer almost immediately.

That does not mean MCQs have become useless.

It does mean that we need to think much more carefully about how we use them.

Today, If an online MCQ assessment is completed unsupervised, what does a high score actually tell us?

Does it demonstrate understanding?

Does it demonstrate good use of resources?

Does it demonstrate engagement?

Or does it simply demonstrate that a student has access to an effective tool?

One possible response is to move everything back into exam conditions.

That might solve part of the problem.

But it also risks losing some of the benefits that made online assessment attractive in the first place.

Today, I do not want to give you a long presentation or pretend that there is one perfect answer.

Instead, I want us to test a series of possible responses to the problem.

We are going to work with five different assessment scenarios.

Some are familiar. Some are more experimental. All of them are technically possible.

For the purpose of the activity, I would like you to assume that the assessment must work with more than 500 students, and that it contributes 40 per cent of the final mark for a 15-credit module.

Your first task is to make the strongest possible case for the scenario that you have been given.

Even if you dislike it, defend it.

Identify its strengths and prepare a short pitch to persuade the rest of the room that it is the best option.

You will then receive a different scenario and prepare an anti-pitch.

Your job will be to challenge it.

Look for weaknesses, unintended consequences, workload problems, fairness issues, and anything else that would make you hesitate before adopting it.

Finally, the original scenario will come back to you, and you will have an opportunity to prepare a rebuttal.

The point is not to win an argument.

The point is to expose the trade-offs in each scenario.

At the end, we will have an individual vote.

Each of you will be able to cast two votes for the scenarios that you find most convincing.

There is no perfect solution.

But doing nothing is also a choice.

Let us see where the discussion takes us.
